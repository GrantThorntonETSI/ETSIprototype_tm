A mark that is widely used by others is not distinctive. A trademark on the Principal Register must be distinctive enough to unambiguously identify the mark owner as the source of the goods or services provided under the mark. If a mark is not inherently distinctive because the wording is descriptive, geographically descriptive, or a surname, the mark owner must show that the mark has acquired distinctiveness through evidence, ownership of a same/similar mark on the Principal Register, or five years’ use.
